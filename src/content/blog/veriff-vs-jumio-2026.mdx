## Veriff Overview

Veriff was founded in 2015 in Tallinn, Estonia. It started as a European identity verification company and has expanded to cover 230+ countries and 10,000+ document types, making it one of the broadest-coverage platforms in the market. Veriff's architecture is AI-first: automated decision engines handle 98%+ of verifications without human review queues, which matters for user conversion rates during onboarding.

The company serves fintech startups, neobanks, gig economy platforms, sharing economy companies, and crypto exchanges. Veriff has particularly deep penetration in European markets, where its eIDAS qualification and GDPR-native architecture give it a compliance edge over US-headquartered competitors.

Certifications tell a meaningful story here. Veriff holds ISO 27001, SOC 2 Type II, eIDAS, and iBeta Level 2. That last certification is the one to pay attention to. iBeta Level 2 is the highest commercial standard for biometric liveness detection under ISO 30107-3. Testing includes sophisticated presentation attacks: 3D-printed masks, high-resolution video replays, and advanced spoofing scenarios. Very few identity verification platforms have achieved Level 2. For companies onboarding users in high-fraud-risk categories (crypto exchanges, high-value lending, gaming), this certification is a meaningful differentiator.

Veriff's 250+ native integrations and hosted verification page option also reduce time-to-production significantly. A non-technical compliance team can get a working Veriff integration live in a week using the hosted page, with no engineering resources required.

**Best for:** Mid-market fintech, scale-ups under 10,000 verifications/month, companies needing strong certifications with accessible pricing, and European operations requiring eIDAS compliance.

## Jumio Overview

Jumio was founded in 2010 in Palo Alto, California, making it one of the oldest automated identity verification companies in the market. That longevity is both a strength and a context clue: Jumio built its business serving Tier 1 banks and large financial institutions before "KYC software" was a common product category.

The company's enterprise positioning is deliberate and consistent. Jumio does not publish production pricing, does not prioritize developer self-service, and does not compete for small-to-mid-market accounts on price. This is not a weakness in Jumio's target market -- it is the correct model for selling into enterprise procurement cycles at major financial institutions.

Jumio's document coverage spans 3,500+ document types across 200+ countries. While that number is lower than Veriff's count, both platforms cover all major global markets with high accuracy. The methodology for counting document types differs between vendors, so the gap is partly definitional.

The certification that sets Jumio apart is FedRAMP. Federal Risk and Authorization Management Program compliance means Jumio meets US federal government security requirements. This makes Jumio viable for US government contracts and for highly regulated US financial institutions that must demonstrate their vendors meet federal security standards. No other major KYC platform can match Jumio on FedRAMP.

Jumio's KYX Platform extends beyond basic identity document verification into orchestrated verification journeys, and includes more AML-adjacent functionality than Veriff's core product.

**Best for:** Tier 1 banks, large enterprises, US government-adjacent financial institutions, and platforms requiring 10,000+ verifications/month with dedicated SLA-backed support.

## Feature-by-Feature Comparison

### ID Document Coverage

Veriff covers 10,000+ document types across 230+ countries. Jumio covers 3,500+ document types across 200+ countries.

The gap in document type count is partly methodological. Veriff counts each document variant separately: a German passport issued in 2019 is a different document type from one issued in 2022, because the security features and layout differ. Jumio's count uses different bucketing. Both approaches are defensible, and both platforms cover the same underlying universe of major global identity documents.

Where the coverage difference is real: Veriff has an advantage in European and Eastern European document coverage, recently issued documents in emerging markets, and edge cases (expired documents, damaged documents, legacy formats from countries that have updated their ID systems). Veriff also processes newer document versions faster -- the AI models update more frequently.

Jumio is well-covered on US documents, major G20 country documents, and high-volume markets. For a US-only financial services company, Jumio's document coverage is fully sufficient.

**Verdict: Veriff has broader official coverage, but both platforms are sufficient for the majority of global use cases.**

### Liveness Detection and Biometrics

This is Veriff's clearest competitive advantage in the Veriff vs Jumio comparison.

Veriff holds iBeta Level 2 certification under ISO 30107-3 -- the highest commercial standard for biometric liveness detection. Jumio holds iBeta Level 1. The difference between Level 1 and Level 2 is not marginal. Level 2 testing includes adversarial scenarios that Level 1 does not: 3D-printed face masks, realistic silicone masks, advanced print attacks, and high-resolution video spoofing. A system that passes Level 2 has been validated against attacks that are commercially available and used by fraud rings targeting financial services onboarding.

Both Veriff and Jumio use passive liveness detection: the user looks at the camera, and the system determines whether a live person is present. No user action is required (no "blink now" or "turn your head"). Passive liveness produces better user conversion rates than active liveness, and both platforms have made the right choice here.

For low-risk verification use cases (shared economy driver onboarding, standard bank account opening), Level 1 liveness is adequate. For high-risk use cases (crypto exchange onboarding, high-value credit applications, age verification for regulated products), Level 2 liveness is meaningfully more secure.

**Verdict: Veriff wins clearly on liveness certification.**

### AML Screening Capabilities

Neither Veriff nor Jumio is a full-service AML platform. Understanding what each platform actually provides here is critical before making a purchase decision.

Veriff's Risk Insights (available on Plus and Premium plans) includes FaceBlock and CrossLinks. FaceBlock flags faces that appear in Veriff's network of previously flagged fraud attempts. CrossLinks identifies accounts that share biometric data across different verification sessions, detecting fraud networks where one person is onboarding multiple synthetic identities. These are fraud detection signals specific to Veriff's biometric network, not AML screening against regulatory databases.

Jumio's KYX Platform includes more AML-adjacent functionality, including some connections to identity risk databases. Jumio has also made acquisitions in the identity risk space. But Jumio is not a sanctions screening or PEP database provider.

Both platforms require integration with a dedicated AML screening tool for full AML compliance: OFAC sanctions screening, EU consolidated sanctions lists, PEP databases, adverse media monitoring. If your compliance program requires this (and most financial services companies under AML regulations do require it), you need a platform like Sumsub or ComplyCube that bundles AML screening, or a standalone AML service alongside your KYC platform.

**Verdict: Tie. Neither platform substitutes for a dedicated AML screening solution.**

### API and SDK Quality

Veriff: REST API, hosted verification page (no-code option for non-technical teams), native iOS and Android SDKs, React Native SDK, Flutter SDK, webhooks for real-time verification notifications, and documentation rated consistently above average in user reviews.

The hosted page option is worth emphasizing. Compliance teams at fintech companies often need to launch a verification flow before engineering bandwidth is available. Veriff's hosted page lets a non-technical person configure a verification flow, generate an embed link, and send it to users -- no API work required. That path from contract to live verification flow can be measured in days.

Jumio: enterprise API, no hosted-page option, complex integration requiring dedicated engineering resources. Jumio's API documentation is enterprise-grade and detailed, but implementation timelines are measured in weeks or months rather than days. This is appropriate for enterprise procurement cycles -- Jumio's customers have implementation teams -- but it is a meaningful disadvantage for any team that wants to move fast.

Veriff also has 250+ native integrations including Salesforce, HubSpot, and Zapier, enabling non-API workflows for compliance and operations teams.

**Verdict: Veriff wins clearly for developer experience and time-to-production.**

### Compliance Certifications

Veriff: ISO 27001, SOC 2 Type II, eIDAS qualified, iBeta Level 2, GDPR compliant, CCPA compliant.

Jumio: ISO 27001, SOC 2 Type II, GDPR, FedRAMP.

Both share the baseline enterprise security certifications (ISO 27001, SOC 2 Type II, GDPR). The differentiators are eIDAS for Veriff and FedRAMP for Jumio.

eIDAS qualification is relevant for European digital identity use cases, particularly in regulated industries that need to meet EU eIDAS Level of Assurance requirements. If you operate in the EU and your compliance program references eIDAS standards, Veriff's qualification is directly relevant.

FedRAMP is relevant for US federal government work and for US financial institutions that must demonstrate their vendors meet federal security frameworks. Banks with federal charters, credit unions, or financial services companies working with US government agencies will find FedRAMP a hard requirement in some procurement contexts.

**Verdict: Tie. The right answer depends on your specific regulatory context and geography.**

### Integrations and Ecosystem

Veriff has 250+ native integrations spanning CRM platforms (Salesforce, HubSpot), automation tools (Zapier), and direct integrations with major fintech infrastructure providers. This means operations and compliance teams can route verification events into their existing workflows without custom engineering.

Jumio's integration ecosystem is built around enterprise API integrations with large platforms, managed through professional services engagements. The breadth of native out-of-the-box connectors is narrower.

For mid-market companies with mixed technical and non-technical stakeholders, Veriff's integration library is a practical advantage. A compliance analyst can set up a Zapier workflow that fires a Slack alert on every failed verification, with no engineering ticket required. Jumio's model assumes you have an implementation team.

**Verdict: Veriff wins for mid-market integration needs.**

### Support and SLAs

Veriff: email support on the Essential plan, progressively better access on Plus and Premium, and a dedicated Customer Success Manager on the Enterprise tier. Self-serve documentation is thorough and consistently well-reviewed.

Jumio: dedicated enterprise support model, SLA-backed uptime guarantees in enterprise contracts, dedicated account management. For enterprise customers who need guaranteed response times written into their contract and a named person responsible for their account, Jumio's support structure is purpose-built for that.

The practical implication: if your company's compliance team needs to escalate an issue at 2 AM during a product launch and you have a contractual SLA, Jumio's enterprise support is structured to handle that. Veriff's enterprise tier has CSM support, but the escalation contractual framework is less formalized for non-enterprise tiers.

**Verdict: Jumio wins for enterprise-tier support quality and SLA contractual guarantees.**

## Veriff vs Jumio Pricing

Veriff publishes pricing openly. Jumio does not.

Veriff's published rates:
- Essential: $0.80 per verification, $49/month minimum
- Plus: $1.39 per verification, $99/month minimum
- Premium: $1.89 per verification, $209/month minimum
- Enterprise: custom pricing

The math for a mid-market company is straightforward. At 500 verifications/month on Veriff Essential, you pay $449/month ($0.80 x 500 + $49 minimum absorb). At 2,000 verifications/month, you pay $1,600/month. Budget forecasting is simple.

Jumio's Starter tier is $0 (sandbox and testing only, not production). The Pro tier at $199/month is a limited production access tier -- not the pricing model that enterprise customers actually use. Production enterprise customers are on annual custom contracts negotiated through Jumio's sales team.

The practical consequence: if your company does fewer than 5,000 verifications/month, Jumio's sales process will not prioritize your account, and you are unlikely to receive competitive pricing through their enterprise sales cycle. Veriff's model was explicitly designed to serve the mid-market that Jumio does not pursue.

For companies above 50,000 verifications/month, enterprise pricing from both vendors is worth requesting. At that volume, Jumio's per-unit pricing may be competitive, and the enterprise support model becomes worth the complexity.

## When to Choose Veriff

Choose [Veriff](/vendors/veriff) if any of these five scenarios describe your situation:

1. **You are a fintech startup or scale-up under 10,000 verifications/month.** Veriff's published pricing means you can model your verification cost as a line item in your unit economics spreadsheet. Jumio's sales process is not designed for your account size.

2. **You need transparent pricing for budget forecasting.** Compliance budget cycles at mid-market companies require concrete numbers. Veriff's pricing structure gives you those numbers before you sign a contract.

3. **You want to go live in days or weeks, not months.** The hosted page option removes the engineering dependency entirely. A compliance manager can configure and deploy a Veriff verification flow without an engineering ticket.

4. **You operate in Europe or need eIDAS qualification.** Veriff's European origins and eIDAS certification make it the natural choice for companies where EU digital identity standards are part of the regulatory requirement.

5. **You need iBeta Level 2 liveness for high-fraud-risk onboarding.** Crypto exchanges, high-value lending platforms, and regulated gaming operators face organized fraud attempts using sophisticated spoofing tools. iBeta Level 2 certification means Veriff's liveness system has been tested against those attacks.

## When to Choose Jumio

Choose [Jumio](/vendors/jumio) if any of these five scenarios describe your situation:

1. **You are a Tier 1 bank or large financial institution processing 10,000+ verifications/month.** Jumio's enterprise model is purpose-built for your procurement cycle, support requirements, and scale.

2. **You need FedRAMP compliance.** If your procurement requirements include FedRAMP authorization, Jumio is currently one of the only identity verification vendors that can satisfy that requirement.

3. **You require enterprise SLAs with contractual uptime guarantees.** Jumio's enterprise contracts include formalized SLA commitments that Veriff's standard tiers do not match.

4. **You have a dedicated enterprise procurement process and can navigate custom pricing.** Jumio's commercial model works well for companies with legal teams, enterprise procurement workflows, and multi-year contract cycles.

5. **You need a dedicated account management team for ongoing compliance requirements.** Heavily regulated financial institutions often need a named person at their KYC vendor responsible for the relationship, regulatory updates, and issue escalation. Jumio's enterprise support model is structured around that expectation.
